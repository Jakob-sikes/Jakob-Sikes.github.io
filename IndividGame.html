UX Case Study

Nursery Nightmare: First-person horror maze game focused on user-centered design. Goal: make navigation and object interaction clear while maintaining suspense and player engagement.

1. Problem

  • Players often felt lost and disoriented in the maze, reducing enjoyment and flow
  • Long hallways caused boredom and slowed pacing; shorter, varied corridors improved suspense and engagement
  • Oversized rooms gave players too much open space, making it difficult to identify points of interest and focus on objectives
  • Lack of visual cues and prompts reduced immersion and clarity
  • Objective: improve spatial understanding, guide interactions, and balance thrill with exploration

2. Research

  • Conducted greybox walkthroughs with 5 participants to observe navigation and interaction patterns
  • Ran full playtests with 10 participants across 5 recorded sessions
  • Collected data on navigation, puzzle engagement, and player focus
  • Insight: players needed clearer cues and prompts, and smaller, more diverse spaces improved engagement
  • Enemy behavior analysis revealed that with only one wandering enemy, encounters were rare, reducing tension in evasion-based gameplay

3. Ideation & Design

  • Shortened most hallways to maintain suspense, while retaining a few long corridors for variety and pacing
  • Redesigned rooms to be smaller and more versatile, allowing multiple approaches to objectives
  • Improved lighting and distinct 3D room assets to help with orientation and navigation
  • Added prompts for interactions ("Square to collect") and environmental cues for guidance
  • Reorganized puzzles and sidequests to support both exploration and the thrill of evading the enemy
  • Considered adjusting enemy behavior or adding secondary enemies to increase thrilling encounters

5. Testing & Iteration

  • Observed player sessions and iterated on maze layout, room sizes, corridor lengths, prompts, and lighting
  • Next playtest scheduled for end of May to validate improvements and measure player engagement with new enemy encounters and sidequests
  • Role: Led UX design, including maze layout, navigation cues, player guidance, and iterative testing

6. Outcome

  • Navigation confusion significantly reduced through shorter, varied hallways and environmental cues
  • Players understood interactions more clearly with prompts
  • Smaller, versatile rooms encouraged exploration, and suspense was maintained with strategically designed corridors and enemy placement

7. Key Takeaways

  • Balancing corridor length and room size is critical to maintain player engagement and suspense
  • Environmental cues and interactive prompts improve navigation without breaking immersion
  • Enemy placement and behavior strongly impact tension; careful design is needed for meaningful encounters
  • Iterative testing with real players informs decisions and uncovers subtle usability issues
